1.1. Metaethics [Palmer 109]

1.1.1. Ronan Ó Maonaile, University of Reading, Why Fit-Making Considerations Are Not Normative Reasons

This paper offers a new argument for the claim that fit-making considerations are not normative reasons. Normative reasons are considerations that count in favour of attitudes or actions. Fit-making considerations - which determine whether an object merits a certain attitude - also count in favour of attitudes, but they provide a distinctive kind of normative support, or so I shall argue. If this is right, then normative reasons are not "first", and fittingness is irreducible to normative reasons.

Fit-making considerations are sometimes (erroneously) called the right kind of reasons. This is in contrast to the wrong kind of reasons. For example, if you threaten to hurt me unless I admire you, I have very good reason to admire you, but this does not make you admirable (the wrong kind of reason). On the other hand, the fact that you are a great philosopher does appear to make you admirable (the right kind of reason). Several authors, including Schroeder (2010), Howard and Leary (2022) and D’Arms and Jacobson (2023) have argued that fit-making considerations just are right-kind reasons.

I first reject two arguments for the claim that fit-making considerations are not right-kind reasons. Schultz (2025) argues that there are no reasons for affective attitudes, right or wrong, because we cannot deliberate to them due to emotional recalcitrance. I argue that recalcitrance shows only that emotional deliberation can fail, not that it is impossible. Maguire (2018) argues that fit‑making considerations lack the weights characteristic of normative reasons. I argue that fit-making considerations plausibly are weighted. This sets up my own argument: fit-making considerations are not normative reasons because while the weighing of reasons issues in three distinct normatively significant verdicts: required, permitted, or forbidden, the weighing of fit-making considerations only issues in two: fitting or unfitting.

1.1.2. Samuele Chilovi, Institute of Philosophy, CSIC, Normative Authority, Formal Normativity, and the Question of What to Do

The aim of this paper is to clarify some key questions in metanormative theory regarding the nature of normative reality and the structure of normative explanation, and to outline some theoretical options addressing them. These questions concern the way the normative realm is structured, how its domains relate to each other, the nature of the normative force they possess, and whether there exists an ultimate notion of authority – of what ought to be the case simpliciter, or all things considered. To do so, I first develop an account of the distinction between formal and authoritative normativity, and then I use it to raise some neglected questions, to systematize existing theories, and to introduce some novel views worthy of serious consideration.

1.1.3. Umut Baysan, University of Southampton, Reasons as Non-Causal Powers

You are a reliable person, which gives me a reason to believe you when you say you are in pain. Likewise, the fact that you are in pain gives me a reason to comfort you (let’s suppose). These are cases in which properties (e.g., being reliable, being in pain) provide us with reasons to respond to the world in various ways. This paper takes as a working assumption that the reason-providing feature of a property is a power of that property.

This assumption makes the reason-providing relation similar to causation in one respect, yet different in another. Consider a case in which a causally efficacious property—such as being negatively charged—confers on its bearer a power, e.g., the power to attract a positively charged particle. This is a causal power; its manifestation consists in a causal change. However, the powers with which this paper is concerned are non-causal powers: their manifestations do not consist in causal changes.

But what does the manifestation of such a power consist in, if not a causal change? I argue that, in relevant cases, these manifestations consist in instantiations of normative relations, such as justification or requirement, which I take to be examples of non-causal explanatory relations. On this proposal, when a property provides us with a reason for action, it confers on its bearers the power to justify or require that action. Importantly, just as a causal power can exist unmanifested (e.g., when it is interfered with), these non-causal powers can also exist unmanifested when the relevant reason is merely pro tanto or defeated.

The paper develops this account by exploring its implications regarding the metaphysics of normativity and value, particularly in relation to the "buck-passing theory of value" and the naturalism/non-naturalism debate in metaethics.

1.1.4. Niklas Kurzböck, University of Bonn, An Essentialist Explanation for the Normative Status of Future Generations

Many accept that we have moral responsibilities to future persons. For example, we ought not to leave a ruined planet for the persons who come after us for no good reason. Now, there are numerous first-order ethical explanations for why we ought not to do so. Many of these explanations rely on the assumption that future persons have a normative status.

However, since these future persons do not yet exist, the non-existence argument challenges this assumption. It does so by arguing on the premise that only actual existing things can have properties. Therefore, future persons cannot instantiate normative properties as they cannot have any properties. The aim of my talk is to reject the actualist premise and, based on an essentialist explanatory model, to argue that future persons have (normative) properties on the same grounds as actual persons.

According to essentialism, the essential properties of an entity explain what it is to be that entity. One important aspect of essential properties is their non-transient character. Given that F is an essential property of x, x must be F at all times, i.e., necessarily, for any stipulated times t1 and t2, if x is essentially F at t1, then x is F at t2. Furthermore, it will be argued for the essential role of normative principles (NP) in essence explanations, which have the form

□ ∀x (F(x) \(\rightarrow\) Nx)

connecting F and a normative property N.

On the basis of this essentialist position, I will argue that it must be true that every (actual and possible) x that is F necessarily is N. Referring to the non-transient character of essential properties, if p is necessarily true of x at t2, then p is also true of x at t1. Therefore, if it is the case at t2 that a future person (x) that is F (e.g., has flourishing conditions) is also essentially N (due to NP), then it must be the case that x is N at t1 (e.g., right now) too.

1.2. Artificial Intelligence [Palmer 102]

1.2.1. Rory Aird, American University of Armenia, ChatGPT isn't safe

Drawing on resources from the analysis of knowledge, in this paper, I argue that ChatGPT is not safe—in the Pritchardian, modal sense. More specifically, I propose that due to inherent and necessary features of the system architecture of large language models involving an element of randomness (known as ‘temperature’), LLM output is analogous to beliefs about lotteries familiar from epistemology; the possibility of error is too modally close (even if probabilistically distant) to be compatible with knowledge. Thus, when asking ChatGPT a question, one can never know the answer solely on the basis of the LLM. I close by suggesting that this result generates something of a zetetic paradox; in order to eliminate the risk inherent in an LLM’s answer to your question, you need further evidence. The only evidence that can play that role, I submit, is already knowing the answer to the very question you ask it.

1.2.2. Rahul Kumar Maurya, Banaras Hindu University, India, Ethics and AI's Challenge to Humanity: A Perspective from Early Buddhism

The conception of ethics has always been central to philosophical deliberations. Philosophy has grown voluminous in response to the age-old question, “How should we live?” This question leads to another question: what is ‘good’? The idea of ‘good’ has been dealt with in religious discourses as the ‘will of the God.’ Though, Plato has challenged this idea of ‘good’ and placed the idea of ‘good’ above God. Ethics, in traditional philosophy, has been looking for metaphysical entities discovered by the mysterious capacity of Reason. Ranging from Plato to Kant, these philosophers were busy bringing forth the universal and absolute moral principles issuing from the metaphysical nature of human beings hidden deep down to the surface appearance of the experiential self.

In this paper, I will try to examine the ethics as envisaged in the religious discourses and the traditional philosophy from the sides of Rorty and early Buddhism. The next step would be to explore the challenges AI is posing to humanity, as Yuval Noah Harari claims that future AI will be capable of making very intimate relations with humans, and by doing so, it can endanger humanity. Further, I would explore whether the Buddhist perspective on ethics can provide a framework towards saving humanity from the accruing threat of AI.

1.2.3. Drew Chambers, Providence College, When should you trust ChatGPT? Deference, Inquiry, and Epistemic Risk

This paper asks whether, and when, it matters for epistemic purposes that a piece of text is generated by AI rather than by a human speaker. I argue that even where AI-generated and human-authored texts are propositionally identical, they are phenomenologically and normatively different in ways that affect their epistemic standing. In particular, extending unquestioning trust to large language model outputs is appropriate only under restricted conditions. The central claim is that the epistemic propriety of trusting AI-generated text varies with features of the inquiry, the kind of content delivered, and the epistemic position of the inquirer. I begin by situating LLM outputs within a broader epistemology of trust. We often rationally place non-deliberative trust in tools and systems whose inner workings we do not understand. If LLMs were merely instruments, then warranted reliance would reduce to reliability. However, LLM outputs are presented in linguistic and dialogical form and carry the normative appearance of assertion. They function, at the level of user experience, more like testimony than like instrumental deliverances. Standard accounts of testimony, however, tie epistemic authority to norms of assertion. Treating LLM outputs as straightforward testimony therefore misdescribes their epistemic character. An alternative would be to take a fictionalist tack, treating LLM outputs as "props" in a game of "make-believe." This approach, however, is just a more sophisticated version of the instrumentalist approach. I propose instead a three-metric framework for evaluating when trust in LLM outputs is appropriate. The relevant metrics are: (1) inquiry type, (2) deliverance type, and (3) inquirer identity. As inquiries become deeper, deliverances more commitment-laden, and inquirers less independently able to assess content, AI-generated text becomes epistemically diminished as a substitute for human sources. The result is a graded model of strong, weak, and non-deference to LLM outputs.

1.3. Normative Ethics [Palmer 103]

1.3.1. Rebecca Brione, King's College London, Requests for permission: an intriguing normative phenomenon hiding in plain sight

We perform requests for permission all the time. However, little attention has been paid to what we do with our words when we request permission. In contrast there is plentiful analysis, although considerable disagreement, on how we characterise responses to requests for permission and what we do when we perform simple requests such as ‘pass the salt’. This dichotomy is striking because permission requests are equally commonplace in everyday life, playing a rich role in ongoing debates about consent, refusal and the conditions for autonomy in people’s lives.

This paper offers a novel characterisation of permission requests. It argues that permission requests generate de novo, content-independent, discretionary reasons for action. That is, they directly generate reasons for the requestee to waive a claim-right, simply ‘because I asked’. The paper shows that analysing permission requests provides novel reasons to support the claim that all requests generate discretionary reasons, rather than obligations, for the requestee to act, and that there are valuable autonomy-related reasons for this.

Permission requests have been overlooked as an important normative phenomenon in their own right. Analysing these important illocutions also supports a more robust characterisation of requests as a whole, and provides a springboard for better understanding their socially-significant sequelae of consent and refusal.

1.3.2. Dong An, Zhejiang University, Moral Bitterness Reconsidered

While moral anger has been extensively discussed in feminist ethics, a closely related emotion, moral bitterness, has received little explicit attention. The few extant theories characterize it with the disappointment of important hopes, helpless anger, or helplessness. I show that each captures part of the phenomenon but none explains its unique psychological structure and moral significance.

Contrary to views that treat bitterness as unresolved anger, I argue that bitterness differs from anger in its communicative orientation. Anger is a second-personal reactive attitude that addresses the wrongdoer, demands uptake, and aims at moral repair. By contrast, bitterness renounces this second-personal address. It expresses blame not by demanding reconciliation or apology but by seeking sympathy or recognition, typically from third parties or from oneself. Recognizing bitterness as a distinct non-second-personal blaming emotion expands our understanding of the reactive attitudes and challenges the assumption that all morally significant blame must be addressed to wrongdoers.

This distinction is further illuminated through the framework of moral repair. Reactive attitudes presuppose hopeful trust in the possibility of restoring moral relationships. Bitterness arises precisely when such hopeful trust is lost. The bitter person continues to judge the wrong as morally significant but no longer aims at resolution. This conceptualization allows bitterness to be understood as an intelligible and coherent response to injustice rather than a psychological defect.

Based on this, I argue that bitterness can be both prudentially and morally appropriate. In contexts of systemic interpersonal injustice, where repair is impossible or prohibitively costly, continuing to feel anger may be psychologically damaging. Bitterness can function as an emotional self-protection. While anger can be admirable for its commitment to moral repair, individuals are not morally obligated to bear its burdens indefinitely. In some cases, withdrawing into bitterness is morally justified.

1.3.3. Pilar Lopez-Cantero, University of Antwerp, Friendship break-ups

Withdrawing from a friendship without explanation has often been regarded as a morally unproblematic decision. This is a stark difference between the moral expectations in romantic love and friendship, which reflects the lower position of friendship in a socially shared hierarchy of intimate relationships. I address the question of whether it is morally permissible to walk away from a friendship without engaging in any meaningful break-up process.

First, I discuss Monika Betzler’s (2024) right and duty to explanation that bind individuals during romantic break-ups. Following my re-formulation of the view from the ethics of care, caring relationships should not end without communication, and without some attempt to mitigate harm. My claim is that insofar as friendships are caring relationships, they constitute moral contexts that require communication at the point of dissolution.

Then, I introduce an important objection. The lack of social scripts for friendship dissolution creates space for the development of alternative, non-normative scripts, such as those emerging in queer communities or in the context of relationship anarchy (Moen & Sørlie 2022). However, it also introduces a distinctive risk. I articulate this risk by drawing on Carme Isern-Mas and Manuel Almagro’s (2025) analysis of “therapy-speak”, understood as the everyday use of psychotherapeutic language outside clinical contexts. Drawing on a widely discussed public case of a friendship break-up articulated through therapy-speak, I argue that such language can be harmful to the person in the receiving end of the break-up, given that it is not conducive to the kind of explanation that is morally required in break-ups: one that is honest, particular, and meaningful. Therapy-speak can then bring about the very harms and wrongs it purports to avoid by contributing to create an oppressive master narrative about friendship break-ups.

1.3.4. Anna Dvorishchina, London School of Economics, Compassion Towards the Culpable

Public discourse often assumes that if an agent is culpable for φing, it is unfitting to feel compassion towards that agent about φing (or something closely related thereto). This is mirrored in the philosophical literature on the topic. Several theorists defend a ‘No-Fault Condition’ on the fittingness of compassion: compassion towards an agent with respect to a setback in her interests is fitting only if she is not at fault for that setback (e.g., Nussbaum). Compassion is fitting respecting a migraine, but not a hangover. I aim to articulate an improved account of the fittingness conditions of compassion that explains why culpability sometimes precludes compassion whilst also identifying conditions under which compassion toward culpable agents remains fitting.

First, I outline the traditional case in favour of the No-Fault Condition and show how it often equivocates between a No-Fault Condition and a No-Desert Condition. This is a mistake. The two conditions are conceptually and extensionally distinct. Second, I argue that these conditions apply respecting specific setbacks and not to persons globally. I then use this claim to show that compassion can be fitting respecting the culpable in two unexpected ways: (1) situations where the wrongdoer’s interests are set back not just by the wrongdoing itself, but also by being the kind of person who would be culpable under the given circumstances and (2) situations where culpability itself is a fitting object of compassion.

I thus defend a nuanced view about the relationship between compassion and culpability. Compassion’s fittingness depends not merely on the agent’s culpability, but on whether the particular setback is faultless and undeserved. Even culpable wrongdoers may be fitting objects of compassion in virtue of aspects of their character or culpability for which they are not responsible, yielding a more precise and attractive theory of compassion toward the culpable.

1.4. Political Philosophy [Palmer 104]

1.4.1. Kieran Oberman, LSE, Nature vs Murder

Should we prioritize saving people from injustice over saving people from mere misfortune? Call the view that we should prioritise injustice over misfortune “Priority”. Philosophers argue that Priority, if true, would have significant practical implications. For instance, Göran Duus-Otterström and Edward A. Page argue that Priority could “change how we think of a range of moral and political debates such as international aid, humanitarian intervention, environmental protection, and health policy”.

But is Priority true? Philosophers have been sceptical. In their view, Priority is vulnerable to counterexamples. These counterexamples seek to model a choice between saving a group of victims from misfortune or saving another group from an injustice, e.g. saving 101 from a lightening fire or 100 from arson. In such examples, it does not seem obvious that we should save the victims of injustice. I shall term these examples “simple conflict cases”.

The philosophical literature thus makes two claims:

  1. Priority, if true, would have significant practical implications.
  2. Simple rescue cases constitute counterexamples to Priority.

This article refutes both claims. Simple conflict cases fail as counterexamples to Priority because they fail to model the misfortune vs. injustice choice they are supposed to model. Consider the lightening vs arson case. If the 101 lightening victims go unsaved, they suffer injustice not misfortune. Why? Because, had the arson not occurred, they would have been saved. The arsonist put them in jeopardy by creating competing victims. They are at risk of becoming the arsonist’s secondary victims. The failure of these counterexamples, moreover, reveals that Priority is of little practical significance. There are, in fact, few cases in which rescuers must choose between saving people from injustice or saving people from misfortune. Most real-world cases are cases of injustice vs injustice.

1.4.2. Gunnar Björnsson, Stockholm University, Distributing the State's Agency: A New Framework for Political Justice

Political philosophy has investigated the normative relevance of distributive ideals concerning the allocation of such things as welfare, rights, opportunities, abilities, resources, and primary goods. Much attention has also been given to formal procedural equality, requiring that everyone’s preferences, welfare, or values be given the same weight in the state’s procedures. In this paper, we argue that the relevance of all these is explained by distributive ideals of a hitherto undertheorized kind: ideals concerning how agents and groups distribute their agency—their attention, deliberation, and action—over time in the service of different individuals’ interests and points of view.

We first illustrate these ideals and clarify the notion of agency distribution through smaller-scale cases: a group of friends consistently acting on the same members’ preferences, parents who focus disproportionately on one child, faculty who engage more deeply with some graduate students than others, and medical research that has historically focused on conditions as they manifest in men.

Applied to the state, the framework grounds distributive responsibilities in the relation between the state’s significant power and its subjects’ dependence on the its operations. Given this relation, the state has special responsibilities to distribute its agency towards its subjects in balanced proportions. This has two central upshots. First, traditional currencies of justice—welfare, primary goods, resources, capabilities—are best understood as instruments for achieving balanced agency distribution given the state’s vast responsibilities, with agency distribution being the more fundamental currency. Second, democracy can be justified as an important instrument for agency distribution, explaining both the intrinsic value of democratic procedures and what is distinctively problematic when persistent minorities fail to influence democratic decision-making: the state consistently fails to act on their points of view.

1.4.3. Marta Giunta Martino, University of Groningen, Can Institutions Express Dissent? An Ontological Perspective on Institutional Moral Protest

"News reports frequently describe institutions as expressing dissent: Harvard refuses to implement directives from the Trump administration, or Apple refuses to comply with court orders demanding access to an iPhone linked to a terrorism case. Expressions of dissent are deliberate, conscientious, and principled acts voicing moral protest. But what does it mean to say that an institution expresses dissent? Are such attributions literal, or merely convenient shorthand for individuals’ actions within institutions?

Philosophical debates tend to assume either that (at least some) institutions count as full-fledged collective agents or that only individual members can express dissent. Both approaches, however, are incomplete. Attributing expressions of dissent to institutions risks mystifying them, as existing accounts of collective agency seem unfit to support morally demanding acts such as conscientious, principled protest, even if they suffice for ordinary coordinated action. Conversely, focusing on individual members overlooks that institutional action is the interrelated actions of rule-governed, embodied roles. While individuals are the units of assessment, coordination is essential for intelligible institutional action. Expressing dissent, moreover, presupposes capacities—judgment and moral discernment—that appear to reside only in members rather than the institution itself.

This paper examines, from an ontological perspective, whether institutions as collective agents can ever express dissent. It analyzes different accounts of collective agency and explores how the interrelatedness of role occupants can structure contexts in which dissent is institutionally intelligible, without invoking a mysterious collective consciousness. Focusing on the dynamics of roles and coordination clarifies how morally significant acts can emerge from institutions while remaining grounded in the capacities of individual members.

Drawing attention to these ontological subtleties, the paper shows why understanding interrelatedness and coordination is crucial for assessing which ethically and politically significant acts, such as expressions of dissent, can be attributed to institutions, and in which circumstances.

1.4.4. Qifan Yang, University College London and University of California, Santa Barbara, Hierarchical Injustice and Immigration

Democratic states usually condemn domestic mobility controls. Yet at borders, the same states require prospective immigrants to petition for admission. This article explains why the immigration hierarchy between citizens and prospective immigrants remains morally troubling even when certain exclusions are permissible. My answer develops a notion of Hierarchical Injustice, grounded in a distinction between two justifications. A vindicatory justification shows that an asymmetry in standing is the vehicle through which a genuine good is realised for the relationally inferior, giving them reason to affirm it from their own standpoint. An excusing justification shows only that the hierarchy is a permissible means to a good realised for someone else. The immigration hierarchy, I argue, may be excused yet never vindicated. This effectively exposes a binary justificatory assumption widespread in migration ethics, on which permissibility exhausts moral assessment. The distinction further resolves an ambiguity in Niko Kolodny's account of objectionable hierarchy and tracks a residual wrong that Iris Marion Young's structural injustice leaves untouched. Citizens accordingly bear an expressive responsibility to acknowledge that wrong. My notion can be extended to other hierarchies sustained by excusing justifications alone, such as the workplace, the family and other domestic civic institutions.

1.5. Aesthetics [Palmer 105]

1.5.1. Mehmet Yıldız, Bilkent University, Context of Distraction: On the Nature of Silly Questions

It often seems as if certain questions about a work of fiction are mere distractions. This paper challenges the view that such an order of questions that are called ‘silly questions’ by Kendall Walton are irrelevant to appreciation and criticism. The aim is (i) to refine the definition of such questions and (ii) to demonstrate the relevance of such questions to appreciation and criticism as the essential markers of comparative proximity, which denotes a reflective attachment to a certain set of presuppositions about a given work of fiction. The paper thereby inherits a variety of questions which have already been deemed distractive by scholars and attempts to clarify the exact reasoning behind such a classification, arguing that a given question constitutes a distractive question if and only if an affirmative answer to each and every one of its constituent positive polar questions gives rise to a conflict with the implications sustained by the context through which a work of fiction is interpreted. Following this definition, the paper presents such a distraction as a prerequisite for attaining an aesthetically enriching awareness of the context through which a work of fiction is interpreted.

1.5.2. Leonardo Nanni, University of Bergamo, An Aesthetic Argument for Supererogatory Actions

Supererogatory actions are those actions that are morally good, although not strictly required. Common moral intuitions suggest that such actions exist: for instance, bringing chocolates to one’s coworkers seems morally good, though it would hardly seem that one is morally required to do it. Despite this, some moral theories argue that supererogatory actions do not actually exist. According to these theories, there are only two types of actions: those that are morally required, and those that are morally wrong, leaving no room for morally good but not required actions. In this presentation, I develop a novel argument for the existence of supererogatory actions. More specifically, I argue that supererogation is needed to explain why some actions possess the aesthetic properties they possess. The argument proceeds in three stages. First, I argue that actions themselves can bear aesthetic properties. For example, the movements of dancers in a classical ballet can be graceful or harmonious when expertly performed, and clumsy or unbalanced when performed poorly. Second, I argue that the aesthetic properties of actions depend on their non-aesthetic properties, and that the latter can include their moral properties. To illustrate, consider a surgeon skillfully removing a patient’s organs: the action may be graceful when performed to save a life, but grotesque if performed to kill the patient for amusement. Finally, I argue that among the moral properties on which the aesthetic properties of actions can depend is the property of being supererogatory. I suggest that the beauty of certain actions-such as extraordinary acts of generosity or self-sacrifice-cannot be adequately explained except on the basis of their being supererogatory. Moral theories that deny the existence of supererogatory actions therefore lack the resources to account for the aesthetic features of some actions, providing reason to reject them.

1.5.3. Tessa Ditner Amorosi, Central Saint Martins, UAL, The Epistemic Impact of an Aesthetic Category

Research on aesthetic categories has long moved beyond the ‘beautiful’ and the ‘sublime’ to consider what has been called ‘minor categories’ such as ‘cuteness’ and its Japanese counterpart ‘kawaii.’ This is often defined as ‘baby schema’ with reference to rounded and sometimes voiceless characters such as Hello Kitty or Snoopy.

I argue ‘cuteness’ is far from just the saccharine and innocuous, as characterised by Walt Disney’s hecklers in the 1900s. It has also been used in official communication to downplay the dangers of nuclear pollution.

I provide a contemporary technology case study to demonstrate the epistemic role of this aesthetic category. It specifically infers domestication where domestication is biologically illogical. The epistemic consequences are the vanishing of safety concerns through inferential meaning.

I use this case study to demonstrate how an aesthetic category can be aesthetically magnetic and link empirical conditions to normative consequences. While a failed use of kawaii has led to public outrage, when wielded effectively it results in a soothing form of silencing.

1.5.4. Colette Olive, University of Leeds, Miserable Characters and Unfeeling Artists: Trauma Porn as an Aesthetic Failing

The term ‘trauma porn’ is increasingly common in the sphere of online art criticism. It crops up in countless Letterboxd reviews and Reddit posts, and is an aesthetic slight. Whilst definitions vary, the term targets an artwork or piece of media that depicts the suffering of a person or group for the sake of entertainment. These depictions of suffering are often perceived as gratuitous or excessive. Amongst the accused stands Hanya Yanagihara’s A Little Life, Darren Aronofsky’s Requiem for a Dream, a significant number of Colleen Hoover’s books, and Tate Tayler’s The Help. Variously, the problem is that such works are dehumanizing, re-traumatizing, objectifying, or exploitative. In each case, the term serves as a moral rebuke. And yet, another, much older genre seems to answer to the description given above: tragedy. Calling a work of art ‘tragic’ does not seem to carry the same moral criticism as calling it trauma porn. 1.15.1. Giovanni Dusi, LOGOS Research Group in Analytic Philosophy in Barcelona, Against the SkShSi structure of competence

The SkShSi hypothesis (HSkShSi) holds that competence—the disposition to succeed reliably—is constituted by an agent's pertinent skill (Sk), the shape (Sh) they are in, and the relevant situation (Si). This view, advanced by Ernest Sosa, is used by many to argue that knowing requires exercising a skill in appropriate conditions. In this article, I shall argue that HSkShSi is incorrect for two primary reasons.

First, the hypothesis is incomplete because it excludes knowledge as a constituent of competence. Using research competence as an example, I highlight that while a broad spectrum of skills (such as formulating research questions and presenting academic findings) is necessary, a competent researcher must also possess knowledge of theoretical constructs in their field, research methods and disciplinary standards. Therefore, by omitting knowledge, HSkShSi does not account for all relevant aspects of competence.

Second, the hypothesis fails to properly differentiate between skill and competence. Most competence research in education and psychology defines competence as encompassing a wide range of domain-specific skills and knowledge, and skill as the ability to perform specific tasks within the domain. In contrast, Sosa equates skill with innermost Sk competence. Hence, HSkShSi does not adequately distinguish crucial facts concerning competence.

The article shows that a skilled but non-knowledgeable agent remains incompetent, implying that knowledge is explanatory prior to competence. Finally, I highlight that an alternative theory of competence must clarify the key defining traits of skill and competence, as well as the necessary and sufficient components of competence.

1.15.2. Krisztian Kos, University of Cambridge, Exploring Attributions of Affective States: The Affective Picture

With diverse effects on our environmental and social interactions, affective states such as emotions and moods have grown increasingly more important for interpersonal relationships and for understanding other people’s minds. What makes up a crucial part of this are our attributions of affective states. These belong to the recently termed “affective stance”: to adopt the affective stance is to ascribe affective states to other people. However, there is no consensus about what is required of a subject to perform accurate ascriptions of affective states. Existing debates surround questions such as: Must a subject have previously experienced the affective state they are attributing, or does it suffice for them to merely possess a concept of that affective state? Do they need to be able to empathise with the other person and, if so, then what type of empathy does this involve?

To answer these questions, I turn to the mechanisms behind the affective stance that enable subjects to adopt this stance in the first place. I argue that there is a certain perspective, which I call the affective picture, that subjects employ in order to adopt the affective stance and which gives them a distinctive epistemic access to other’s affective states. To do this, I outline the affective picture and its various parts, such as the perspective-taking it involves, its second-person dimension, and its dispositional nature. Next, I use this account of the affective picture to address the above questions surrounding concept-possession and empathy, and offer conditions under which subjects can accurately attribute affective states to others. Then, I detail its relationship with the affective stance and, in doing so, give a more detailed account of the affective stance. After addressing some objections, I end by considering what disruptions to the affective picture can reveal about cognitive disorders and social cognition.

1.15.3. Shunsuke Sasaki, University of Tokyo, Toward a Unified Account of Survival Criteria through Interventionist Causality

Derek Parfit’s Reductionism holds that what matters in survival is not personal identity but Relation R — psychological connectedness and continuity sustained by the “right kind of cause.” Yet that cause has remained notoriously underspecified: Parfit’s candidates — the “normal,” “reliable,” or “any” cause — rest on no principled criterion, leaving it unclear which future being qualifies as a survivor. I argue that James Woodward’s interventionist theory of causation supplies the missing rigour. On this theory, a relation counts as causal to the degree that it is invariant under intervention — it continues to hold across a range of interventions on the relevant variables. Using this notion, I define the causal connectedness one entity bears to a future entity as such an invariant relation, its strength fixed by the degree of invariance, and causal continuity as overlapping chains of connectedness. On the resulting Interventionist Criterion of Survival, a subject survives just in case some future entity is strongly causally continuous with it. Because survival here tracks causal dependence, not bare resemblance, the mere existence of a coincidental duplicate does not constitute the subject’s survival.

This criterion in turn yields a unifying meta-theory. Because interventionist causation is defined over a chosen set of variables, applying this framework to a person requires modelling her as such a set, and different choices generate different verdicts. Building the model from psychological variables recovers a broadly Lockean criterion; from biological or organismic variables, animalism; from variables of material constitution, a bodily criterion. This model-dependence is not a defect but a resource: it lets us redescribe the major theories of survival as differing selections of variables within one framework. On this picture, their long-standing dispute looks less like a clash of rival metaphysics than a choice of what to model — locating “what matters” in a precise, naturalistic theory of causation.

1.15.4. Daniel Cristian Stancu, University of Bucharest, Faculty of Philosophy, Focusing on Actions to Disperse the Distinction between Minimal Self And Narrative Self

In the last decades, philosophers and cognitive scientists working on the self have tried to depart from the Cartesian perspective (self as a substantial entity). The minimal and the narrative approach have raised a conundrum: The minimal self approach argues that if we give up on all superfluous aspects of the self, there remains an immediate something which we are inclined to (still) call a self. The narrative approach argues that what we call self is a narrative construct, linking discrete experiences into a coherent whole. The tension between the two arises from competing views when it comes to the constitution of selfhood: which one mediates or is mediated by the other? Recent work (Brandon, 2016; Køster, 2017; Heersmink, 2018; Dings, 2019; Fanti Rovetta, 2024) shifted the discussion, arguing that embodiment and narrative entail a twofold process. However, I reckon that the attempt mentioned above does not provide a solution. In my view the solution is to dissolve the distinction, by moving the focus from types of selves and their interaction, onto agents engaged in actions within their world.

1.16.1. Janine Gühler, Pembroke & Worcester Colleges, University of Oxford, Frege on the Objectivity of Numbers and Dependence on Reason

In the Grundlagen der Arithmetik Frege claims that numbers are self-subsistent objects, i.e. object that exist independently of anything else. Frege’s Grundlagen §26 ends with the claim that what is objective is independent (unabhängig) of sensation, intuition and imagination but not of reason (Vernunft). Those who ascribe Idealism to Frege have taken this statement in support of the view that Frege believed that the existence of abstract objects such as numbers depends on reason. I suggest that this misunderstands Frege’s point. The same §26 clarifies earlier that to be grasped or understood (Erkanntwerden) is not to be created (Entstehen), which supports a Realist interpretation. The objectivity of the Earth axis is not fictitious or devised (erdacht) or created (entstanden) but simply thought (gedacht). Otherwise, Frege claims, we would not be able to assert anything about the Earth axis before it was thought of by us. While Frege does not speak explicitly of the independence (Unabhängigkeit) from being thought of the Earth axis, I believe his talk of not being created clearly denotes ontological independence of what is objective. When Frege speaks of the dependence on reason of what is objective, I suggest he does not denote ontological dependence but rather epistemological or semantic dependence. If what is objective were to depend ontologically on reason, it is hard to see how Frege can at the same time maintain that thought does not create what is objective. Rather, Frege wants to say that while what is objective does not depend on individual points of view or different ways of perception, any statement or thought that says something about number depends on reason since reason is necessary for the formation of a thought. I therefore suggest that Frege makes a trivial point at the end of §26 rather than an ontologically loaded one.

1.16.2. Francesco Gandellini, The Hebrew University of Jerusalem, Identity or inseparability? Spinoza meets Wittgenstein

In the Ethics, Spinoza famously claims that ‘[t]he order and connection of ideas is the same as the order and connection of things’ (2p7). Interpreters agree that this proposition asserts the existence of a parallelism between thought and being. In the scholium to 2p7’s demonstration, the philosopher further adds that ‘a mode of extension [i.e., a being in the world] and the idea [i.e. thought] of that mode are one and the same thing, but expressed in two ways’ (italics mine). In light of these claims, Spinoza is often read as endorsing the strong thesis that thought is identical with being, and thus as anticipating a view traditionally associated with Hegel. Scholars debate how Spinoza motivates this identity. Michael Della Rocca has forcefully argued that the proof for 2p7 is driven by Spinoza’s uncompromising commitment to the Principle of Sufficient Reason (‘PSR’), according to which every fact must have an explanation. If so, no fact can be brute or unexplained. In this paper, after introducing the key ingredients of Spinoza’s proof of 2p7, I examine Della Rocca’s reconstruction on the basis of the PSR. I then argue that Della Rocca’s argument does not, in fact, establish the identity of thought and being that he and others attribute to Spinoza. A different claim follows instead, namely one of inseparability between thought and being, which I proceed to illustrate. Not only does this claim actually results from employing the PSR in the way Della Rocca proposes, but is also avoids a further brute bifurcation – ruled out by the PSR itself – to which Della Rocca seems committed. By elucidating this claim of inseparability, I finally suggest that Spinoza’s position is better understood as anticipating a view about the alignment of thought and world akin to that found, for instance, in (the later) Wittgenstein.

1.16.3. Kaj André Zeller, University of Leeds, How to Handle the Problem(s) of Dimensional Poverty

Naive non-cognitivism provides an account of moral psychology that deserves to be defended for its simplicity. According to this view, the basic evaluative attitude is a simple desire: an intrinsic first-order desire, without any further accompanying attitude being part of the basic evaluative attitude. However, naive non-cognitivists face the Problem of Dimensional Poverty: simple desires appear too dimensionally poor to be the basic evaluative attitude. It has been overlooked that the Problem of Dimensional Poverty can be distinguished into different versions. The formal version is concerned with schematic representations of mental attitudes. The substantial version is concerned with accounts of particular dimensions of an attitude. I propose two ‘moves’ (the ‘adverbialisation move’ and the ‘deflation move’) that naive non-cognitivists can appeal to when replying to what I call the ‘easier’ formal and substantial problems. I then develop a ‘harder version’ of both the formal and the substantial problem. I suggest that answering the harder formal problem requires adopting an attitude-based scholastic account of desire, according to which desires are attitudes of presenting-as-good. To answer the harder substantial problem, the naive non-cognitivist might appeal to a functional or phenomenological characterization of the dimensions of the basic evaluative attitude.

1.16.4. Will Patterson, King's College London, Does 'Why?' objectify agency? A defence of Anscombe's Intention

"In Intention (1957/2000), Elizabeth Anscombe defined intentional actions as those to which “a certain sense of the question ‘Why?’ is given application” (I, 9). Recently, Anton Ford (2017) has challenged her proposal to understand intentional actions in terms of the question ‘Why?’. Anscombe proposes to define intentional actions in terms of how they are explained (ibid., 219). This, Ford contends, threatens disastrous consequences for our understanding of human action: a focus on action explanation leads us to privilege the standpoint of those who observe, and demand explanation for, intentional actions; leading us to neglect the standpoint of the agent herself as she carries out her action, forcing us to objectify human agency (ibid., 228-230). As a corrective, he suggests that we focus on the question ‘How?’, rather than ‘Why?’, bringing into view an agent’s order of calculation about what to do, and revealing “the structure of what one thinks insofar as one is acting.” (ibid., 227)

I reply to Ford’s worry on Anscombe’s behalf. I draw on some resources from Anscombe’s account of practical truth to argue that, for Anscombe, we develop practical rationality by aiming to act in a way that is practically true; where acting in a way that is practically true is acting in a way that satisfies the description ‘doing well’ (PT, 152). To do that, I suggest, one must grasp why some action satisfies the description ‘doing well’, which means that a practically rational agent must be interested in why some action should be performed, as well as how to perform it. To that extent, the question ‘Why?’ does reveal the structure of what one thinks insofar as one is acting, for Anscombe; so, her focus on the question ‘Why?’ does not objectify agency. "

2.1.1. Emily Thomas, Durham, Letters of Women Philosophers 1902-1951: Sexism and Shadow Networks

This talk explores connections between six philosophers: E. E. Constance Jones, May Sinclair, Karin Costelloe-Stephen, Hilda Oakeley, Susan Stebbing, and Margaret Macdonald. They worked within seemingly disparate movements - British idealism, Bergsonism, early analytic - yet I’ll use archival letters to show they belonged to criss-crossing philosophical networks. These letters are due to be published May 2026 (Letters of English Women Philosophers 1902-1951, OUP). Having briefly introduced all six figures, this talk will explore two issues arising from their correspondence: sexism, and shadow networks.

Challenges. All six figures faced significant challenges - including sexism. For example, when Jones writes to Bertrand Russell for permission to attend his all-male 1910 logic lectures, she was conscious of the need to bring a chaperone. In 1937, Macdonald reports she has not applied for a position at Swansea for the university council would not ‘contemplate appointing a woman’. In 1939, Gilbert Ryle commented on Stebbing’s suitability for a Cambridge chair, ‘Of course, everyone thinks you are the right person to succeed Moore, except that you are a woman’.

Shadow networks. Many of our six philosophers knew each other well, yet not a single letter has survived between them. This is probably because (unlike some of their male peers) their philosophic correspondence has not been preserved in a conscious way. Nonetheless, what does survive reveals shadowy personal links between them. They wrote each other employment reference, attended their talks, visited. Going beyond their correspondence, the records of the Aristotelian Society show many discussions through which they interacted. This talk will detail the personal connections between our six women and - going further - sketch the broader women’s networks they belonged to. We’ll see that these women philosophers used their interpersonal networks to address some of the challenges they faced.

2.1.2. Fiona Woollard, University of Southampton, An argument for the unequal distribution of parental duties after birth

It is normally assumed that those concerned with justice within the family should fight for equal parenting roles and equal parental duties. We should reject these assumptions: in many cases, justice demands unequal distribution of parental duties after birth. Gestational parents face distinctive burdens during pregnancy and birth. Non-gestational parents owe compensation to gestational parents for these distinctive burdens. By default, this results in a redistribution of parental duties amongst parents: non-gestational co-parents acquire a duty to the gestational parent to take on a greater share of the post-pregnancy burdens. I also discuss two cases which, like pregnancy and birth, involve a kind of body-sharing: lactation and bodily tissue/organ donation. I argue that non-gestational parents have additional reasons, but not duties, to induce lactation where possible. On my view, parents have defeasible duties to donate bodily tissues and organs if needed by their child. If so, then compensation for the burdens of pregnancy puts non-gestational parents first in line for such duties. If parents do not have defeasible duties to donate, then non-gestational parents have additional reasons to take on this task.

2.1.3. Bengü Demirtas, Bilkent University, Sexual Consent as a Standing Normative Relation in Enlightenment Philosophy and Marriage Law

Contemporary debates on sexual consent focus on the permissibility of particular sexual acts. In contrast, in Enlightenment England, the marital contract functioned as the primary medium through which sexual rights and obligations were discussed, both legally and philosophically. I argue that the marriage contract served as a non-act-specific sexual consent implying the subordination of the wife's sexual and otherwise agency within the legal personhood of the husband. Consequently, consent to specific sexual actions was not a point of contention within marriage. Consent to the marriage contract entailed consent to a collection of on-going correlative actions and relationships that the marriage contract allowed. As a result, talking about women’s sexual agency was conceptually difficult and only possible with reference to a cluster of actions associated with marriage and the family as a whole. Despite this conceptual difficulty, Mary Wollstonecraft and Mary Astell emphasized the limited agency ascribed to women within and outside of the marital institution through discussions of women’s vulnerability to sexual and other forms of violence. Building on feminist critiques of the social contract tradition (Pateman, 1989; Mackinnon, 1989), this article analyzes the marital contract as presupposing a type of sexual consent that consists in permission to a normative framework regulating sexual rights and obligations, rather than to individual acts. Through a transdisciplinary analysis that combines legal doctrine with moral philosophy, I argue that the early modern and Enlightenment non-act-specific sexual consent which operates through the marital contract anticipates the modern act-specific conception by stipulating the conditions under which sexual permission is granted. The recognition of this structural continuity between Enlightenment and modern conceptions of sexual consent will be especially helpful in diagnosing the strengths and weaknesses of contemporary act-specific consent models, particularly in cases where power relations dictate the background conditions of one’s ability to grant permission.

2.1.4. Christian Meyer, Freie Universität Berlin, Radical Feminist Conceptions of Sexual Liberation

"This paper offers a philosophical reconstruction of radical feminist debates about sexual liberation during the 1970s and 1980s, debates sometimes termed the “feminist sex wars”. The emerging picture provides an intervention in current feminist debates about sexual freedom. A recent wave of feminist work has criticized liberal notions of sexual freedom by recovering elements of antipornography feminism, an outgrowth of radical feminism. I argue that we should rather turn to early radical feminism and sex-radical feminism, another off-shoot of radical feminism, to develop a politics of sexuality beyond liberalism. Such a politics closely connects sexual liberation with the ambition to abolish gender as we know it.

To start, I show that various opposing parties within radical feminism, including both antipornography feminism and sex-radical feminism, shared a commitment to politicizing sexuality, rejecting a liberal sexual politics that assigned sexuality to the realm of the private. Sexuality, they all argued, was closely connected to gender: gender-conformity was stabilized by an imperative to sexual conformity, and sexual behaviour was policed regarding its gender-conformity. This entanglement of sexuality and gender implied that the abolition of gender was a condition for sexual liberation. Antipornography feminists drew the conclusion that sexual liberation would have to wait until ‘after the revolution’, leaving them without an effective sexual politics for the present. Sex-radical feminists, by contrast, went on to develop prefigurative strategies of disentangling gender and sexuality by locally decoupling sexuality from real-world power. Importantly, sex-radical gender abolitionist politics was emphatically inclusive of sexual minorities, contrasting with some antipornography feminism. During the 1980s, both sides eventually abandoned an analysis of sexual liberation that was connected to the ambition of abolishing gender, leaving this vision of sexual politics a path not taken. I argue that this development weakens feminist analyses of sexual liberation until today.

2.2. Philosophy of Language [Palmer 109]

2.2.1. Rhiannon Snook, University of Reading, Ay Up Me Duck! On Written Representations of Linguistic Variation

Linguistic variation has long been investigated by sociolinguists, and more recently, a topic of interest for philosophers of language, with regards to how differences in the meaningful form of an utterance alters the information relayed, and the agency and performative abilities of the speaker (Camp, Nowak, 2025). There is more than one way of saying the same thing, different conversational contexts have different linguistic expectations, and linguistically varying utterances carry differing social significances even if their essential content is the same. We navigate these differences all the time, often without realising, and rely on our knowledge of how certain differences in speech (e.g. accent, register, colloquialisms) are perceived socially to understand their significance. In this paper, I will argue that when it comes to written representations of linguistic variation, whilst there is a lack of richness to the information relayed in “written speech” that is gained from a conversational context, the written “speakers” are still held to the same scrutiny, and oftentimes criticised more than their vocal counterparts when depicting variation. These criticisms, in turn, influence the perceptions of the speakers within a social context, reinforce often negative, sometimes inappropriate, stereotypes, and limit the linguistic abilities of these speakers.

2.2.2. James Kirkpatrick, University of Oxford and Magdalen College, Oxford, A Potentialist Theory of Natural Language

Philosophy of language and generative linguistics traditionally assumes that natural languages are countably infinite sets of well-formed sentences. However, it is not obvious that modelling languages as completed infinite sets is required to explain our finite, yet unbounded linguistic competence, and underappreciated mathematical results suggest that the collection of well-formed sentences vastly exceeds merely countable infinity. In this paper, I develop a novel theory — the potentialist theory of natural language — according to which a natural language is never a completed infinity, but an indefinitely extensible structure. New sentences can always be formed, yet there is no final stage in which all possible sentences are present. I formalize this thesis in a modal framework, using Kripke models to represent stages of linguistic development, and reinterpret formal grammars within this potentialist semantics. The resulting framework reframes the size debate as a question of unending linguistic growth, thus offering a new perspective on recursion, generative capacity, and the metaphysics of language.

2.2.3. Artur Kosecki, Uniwerystet Szczecinski, An Austinian Account of Lexicalized Concepts: Responding to Strawson's Challenge in Conceptual Engineering

Strawson’s Challenge (Strawson 1963; Cappelen 2018) draws attention to one of the methodological issues in conceptual engineering: how to determine whether a conceptual revision preserves the original concept or instead amounts to changing the subject. Much of the recent literature locates conceptual continuity either in semantic content (Cappelen 2018) or in evaluative and functional aims (Queloz 2022; Köhler and Veluwenkamp 2024). This paper argues that neither strategy offers a fully satisfactory, non-arbitrary criterion of conceptual identity across conceptual change.

The paper develops an Austinian account of lexicalized concepts that shifts the focus of continuity from metasemantics to pragmatics (see Austin 1962). On this view, conceptual continuity is not grounded in stable meanings or prescribed functions, but in historically reproduced lineages of pragmatic roles enacted within norm-governed speech-act practices (Sbisà 2009; Witek 2015, 2022). Conceptual continuity is maintained when a revised concept continues to perform the same illocutionary role within these practices, even as interpretive practices, felicity conditions, or uses develop.

On this approach, Strawson’s Challenge is recast as a question concerning the persistence of social and institutional practices rather than one concerning the sameness of stable meaning. To illustrate its explanatory potential, the paper considers the legal concept of woman, showing how a historically sustained pragmatic lineage can support conceptual continuity through significant legal and social change. By contrast, the shift from invalid to disabled person is treated as a case of conceptual discontinuity, in which an established pragmatic lineage is deliberately disrupted, helping to clarify the distinction between conceptual revision and conceptual replacement.

The paper’s main contribution is a Pragmatic Continuity Criterion intended as a diagnostic tool for conceptual engineering. By providing a way of assessing whether a proposal concerns the same concept prior to normative evaluation, the account aims to clarify questions of conceptual identity while remaining neutral with respect to subsequent ethical and political assessment.

2.2.4. Hugo Heagren, King's College London, Divided speech acts

Suppose you write a book, which takes you two years, and later someone asks when you wrote it. You might say 'from 2011 to 2012'. And indeed, at some points in 2011 and in 2012, you were writing. At other times you were researching, making notes, editing, and negotiating with the publisher. But at some times in this interval you were eating, sleeping, driving, and doing all the other mundane things one must do. Writing the book took two years, but you didn't spend all of two years writing the book. Writing the book is what I call a 'divided' act. It is an act made up of multiple constituent acts (an hour of research, ten minutes of writing, etc.), each of which is disjoint from the others. Writing the book takes two years, even though no one of these constituent acts takes more than a few hours. Divided acts are very common: writing a book, getting a degree, learning a language, building a house, etc. are all divided acts. I claim that some speech acts are divided acts, and that considering them as such is more philosophically enlightening than as merely speech acts. In particular, taking certain speech acts to be divided explains how acts which can only happen over a long period of time, or with many agents' joint effort can still count as speech acts, though paradigm speech acts are signal utterances by single agents. I describe divided speech acts in more detail, and show that hinting is an example of speech which is best analysed as divided.

2.3. Sex and Consent [Palmer 102]

2.3.1. Andrés (Andy) Garcia, Lund University, Love Drunk: Asymmetric Infatuation and the Ethics of Sex

This paper explores how asymmetric passionate infatuation creates ethically precarious conditions for sexual encounters. While such encounters may involve valid consent, the effects of passionate love (including obsessive focus, emotional dependency, altered priorities, and diminished cognitive control) create vulnerability and power imbalances analogous to those present in intoxicated sexual encounters. Drawing on neuroscientific research linking romantic love to addiction-like neural patterns and reduced capacity for critical reflection, I argue that infatuation can compromise our ability to recognise risks, assert boundaries, and make decisions aligned with our stable values.

Crucially, my claim is not that passionate infatuation invalidates consent. Rather, I distinguish between two questions in sexual ethics: whether conditions render someone incapable of consenting, and whether conditions make an encounter ethically precarious even when consent remains valid. I focus on the latter. Just as we recognise that a sober person engaging sexually with an intoxicated partner faces moral obligations beyond securing consent, so too does the less-infatuated party in an asymmetric romantic context. They are better positioned to recognise their partner's vulnerability and should exercise corresponding care.

The asymmetry is key. When both parties are equally infatuated, the ethical risks diminish: neither is positioned to exploit the other's altered state, and the power dynamics are balanced. But when one party is deeply infatuated while the other is not, the less-affected party holds disproportionate power and bears moral responsibility to acknowledge this.

I conclude by considering practical implications. Cultural norms that romanticise passionate intensity obscure these risks and discourage critical reflection. Consent education could be broadened to address how affective states, not only external substances, can create ethical precarity, helping individuals recognise and communicate the impact of emotional context on intimate decisions.

2.3.2. Milena Bartholain, Goethe-Universität Frankfurt, Sexual Paternalism or the Right to Be Wronged Sexually

Sometimes we refuse a sexual invitation or request on the grounds that accepting it would not be good for the inviter. Such refusals are plausibly paternalistic. Yet many such refusals also seem morally appropriate. I call these cases permissible private sexual paternalism and argue that they generate two questions. First, are these refusals genuinely paternalistic, given that it is always appropriate to refuse a sexual invitation? Second, if they are paternalistic, why are they permissible, when they are?

I address the first question by situating sexual negotiation within the framework of open-call speech acts, such as invitations, offers, requests, and permissions (Caponetto 2023; Kukla 2023). A defining feature of open-call speech acts is that refusal is always appropriate. At the same time, standard autonomy-based accounts of paternalism hold that paternalistic behaviour necessarily involves some infringement of the subject’s autonomy. But, if no one has a right that their sexual invitation be accepted, how could a refusal wrong their autonomy? I argue that paternalistic refusals of open-call speech acts wrong the subject’s autonomy because the paternalistic agent aims to control via their refusal a subject matter that properly falls within the subject’s domain of authority, such as their sex lives.

I address the permissibility puzzle by defending a non-standard framework according to which paternalistic permissions are determined by how conflicts between rights and their corresponding duties are resolved. On this view, paternalistic refusals in sexual contexts are permissible when, and because, the agent’s negative duty not to harm the other outweighs the other’s right to waive their non-interference rights. In sum, permissible paternalistic refusals of sexual invitations disrespect the person’s right to place an invitation that the paternalistic agent is not allowed to accept.

2.3.3. Talita Ferrantelli, LSE, Defeating Sexual Consent

Consent plays a valuable role in our lives. The possibility of consent allows us to shape the normative significance of our relationships and, where consent is given, it enables us to engage in activities that would otherwise be impermissible. But what if we are systematically not in a position to know whether consent is functioning in the proper way when exercising its power? In this paper, I show that in the special case of sexual consent, there is a fundamental epistemic fragility concerning the possibility of consent serving our normative and practical interests. I argue that the morally transformative power of consent in sexual contexts is especially vulnerable to epistemic defeaters – i.e. conditions that block consenters from knowing whether consent is shaping the normative status of what is done to them and guiding the deliberation of their sexual partners. The result is that sexual consent often does not realise its full potential in the sexual sphere. The moral power of sexual consent is systematically defeated in ways that often make it irrelevant to regulate the sexual sphere.

2.3.4. Peter Schaber, University of Zurich, Legitimate Threats

Various authors argue that illegitimate threats invalidate consent, whereas legitimate threats do not. I will not question this view. Instead, I ask what, precisely, distinguishes a legitimate threat from an illegitimate one. When is a threat illegitimate? When, if ever, is it legitimate?

Some argue that threats are never legitimate. Others hold that they are legitimate whenever the person who threatens has a right to perform the threatened act. Still others doubt that this is always the case, claiming that legitimacy attaches not to all permissible threatened acts but only to some of them. I argue in this paper that a threat is legitimate if and only if the threatened act is morally permissible, and that the threatened act is morally permissible only if it is an appropriate response to the threatened person’s refusal to perform the desired act. I further argue that it is an appropriate response only if the threatened person has a duty to perform the act in question.

2.4.1. Karol Polcyn, University of Szczecin, Conceivability Intuitions and A Posteriori Physicalism

There is no consensus on how to explain our intuitive resistance to a posteriori physicalism. While physicalists argue that this resistance is due to a cognitive error of some sort (cf. Papineau 2002, 2020; Sundström 2018), I offer a different diagnosis. A posteriori physicalism is intuitively unintelligible, even if it is true, and this unintelligibility is not the result of an error. The source of the problem lies in an epistemic gap between phenomenal truths and physical truths, namely, the conceivability of zombies. As I argue, there are two ways in which the conceivability of zombies renders a posteriori physicalism an unintelligible view.

First, physicalists are not able to explain why zombies are conceivable, consistently with their commitment to the impossibility of zombies. According to physicalists, it is consistent to hold that zombies are impossible, because it is consistent to hold that phenomenal concepts refer to physical properties despite being very different from physical concepts (cf. Papineau 2002, 2020). But if it is consistent to hold that consciousness is physical, how could one conceive of a scenario in which our physical duplicates are not conscious? It seems that one would have to conceive that consciousness is not itself, which is absurd.

Second, while a posteriori identities are in general deducible from the complete description of the world which does not involve identities (Chalmers and Jackson 2001), a consequence of the conceivability of zombies is that psychophysical identities are not deducible from the complete physical description of the world. One can therefore assume, consistently with our complete physical knowledge, that the relation between conscious states and physical states is mere correlation rather than identity. And for this reason, it is arbitrary to assume that conscious states are identical with physical states as opposed to being merely correlated with them.

Epistemically, what is familiar is just what is “known,” “well known,” or even “intimately known.” Yet the feeling of familiarity can become an unresolved mystery when its source cannot be placed or recalled. In memory science, familiarity is operationalized either as a primacy variable or as recognition without identification. In these contexts, the contrast to familiarity is novelty. There is also a philosophical literature in which familiarity is treated more expansively, encompassing what is “ordinary” or “everyday.” Here the contrast to familiarity is not mere novelty but oddity or uncanniness. This emphasizes the additional phenomenological dimension.

I approach familiarity as a constituent of the first-person perspective, as an element in “subjectivity.” Certainly, subjective consciousness is necessary for familiarity (cf. Montemayor, 2024). A robot or zombie will re-identify patterns of information without finding them familiar, much as it discriminates frequencies of light without experiencing color. I characterize familiarity as minimal autonoesis, comparable to “chronesthesis,” which is a component of the Feeling of Pastness (Neisser, et al, 2023; Perrin, et al, 2020). Familiarity is a call to memory. In cognitive psychology, this can be approached via the “familiarity-flip-of-attention” hypothesis (Cleary, et al, 2025), which holds that familiarity flips attention inward, toward recall. I argue that this model also helps explain the relation between consciousness and self-consciousness. In familiarity without recall, the mystery of the familiar prompts us to look more deeply into ourselves.

In the phenomenological tradition, subjectivity is sometimes defined in terms of familiarity (Frank, 2012). This definition is subtly mistaken, but something can be learned from it about the relation between conscious memory and the self.

2.9.2. Alon Isac, University of York, Pain's Normative Constraints

Despite pain’s phenomenal, neuronal and functional idiosyncrasy, there have been recent attempts to identify a property or set of properties shared by all pains, in virtue of which they are considered pains. In this paper, I argue that such attempts should be rejected. Instead, I suggest that pain is a family resemblance concept constrained by pragmatic and moral considerations. On this view, pains share a range of phenomenal, neuronal and functional properties in a crisscrossing and overlapping manner, rather than a primal pain property or element. However, since many of these properties are also present in non-painful experiences, their presence alone cannot determine whether an experience is painful. I argue instead that pain categorization is guided by practical goals and ethical values. Accordingly, categorizing pain involves not only the identification of common properties, but also evaluating their importance. Pain research should therefore acknowledge that normative judgements are constitutive of pain.

2.9.3. William Gopal, University of Glasgow, Expertise and Norms of Attention

Being able to offer a precise line of criticism of professional experts (e.g., scientists, doctors, lawyers, engineers, etc.) who present true but distorting information to non-experts is pressing. In this paper, I identify some of the normative demands generated if we assume professional experts perform a function for non-experts; namely, that professional experts perform the role of a sentinel insofar as they make possible threats emerging from their domain of expertise salient to non-experts. At first glance, a promising line to cash out the normative demands of professional experts is to suggest that beholden to more demanding epistemic responsibilities when interacting with non-experts in this way. For example, that when playing the role of a sentinel, professional experts ought to testify qua expert only within their field of expertise; or act with humility by, for example, admitting to inaccuracies or sincerely responding to competing views whether presented by another expert or a non-expert ( Gerken, 2018; Ballantyne, 2019; Zagzebski, 2024). On this picture, what a professional expert ought to do when acting as a sentinel is, at least partially, set by alethic concerns. However, depending on what is made salient – and what one attends to – information can be true yet distorting (Gardiner, 2022). If that’s the case, then it’s possible that, while functioning as a sentinel, professional experts can satisfy their more demanding epistemic responsibilities by, for example, presenting only true claims, yet nonetheless mislead non-experts in a way that warrants criticism. The upshot of this paper, is a view that professional experts are beholden to specific norms of attention, which in turn, offers a line of criticism against those who present true – but distorting – information to non-experts, unavailable with the current conceptual apparatus in social epistemology.

2.10.1. Karl Landström, Nottingham Business School, The Wrongs and Harms of Epistemic Exploitation

Exploitation, a recurring concern in both ethics and political philosophy, has recently garnered increasing attention among philosophers of science and epistemologists. Exploitative knowledge-practices are being explored under the guise of epistemic exploitation, and a number of accounts detailing the harms and wrongs of such exploitation have been developed. However, these accounts diverge in normatively significant ways on both the central wrong of, and the harms associated with, epistemic exploitation. Further, I will argue, extant accounts of epistemic exploitation remain too narrow to appropriately capture the diverse ways in which epistemic practices can become exploitative. My primary aim in this paper is to develop a conception of epistemic exploitation that can capture this diversity, while at the same time account for the harms and wrongs of epistemic exploitation that have been identified in the extant literature. At the same time, I aim to re-conceptualise the central wrong of epistemic exploitation, to insulate it from challenges based on the nonworseness claim.

In order to do so, I examine the extant literature on epistemic exploitation and of exploitative research practices to illustrate the diversity in how these accounts conceive of what makes epistemic exploitation wrong, and the primary harms that such practices produce. Following that I draw on longstanding debates in applied ethics to introduce the nonworseness claim as a challenge to existing accounts of epistemic exploitation. In light of this challenge, I argue that any satisfactory conception of epistemic exploitation ought to be able to account for both mutually beneficial and structural forms of exploitation. In addition, I argue that such an account ought to be able to handle the normative questions that arise as a result of the complex choice situations facing the exploited. Having identified these desiderata, I begin developing such an account centred around claims of inferiority, drawing on recent work in political philosophy. I conclude the paper by briefly discussing the implications of this account of epistemic exploitation for some of the solutions and mitigating strategies that have been proposed to address exploitative epistemic practices in science and education, including extractivist research practice.

2.10.2. Dominik Jarczewski, Jagiellonian University (Krakow), On Epistemic Respect

I propose to develop epistemic respect as a distinct normative category for evaluating epistemic practices and beliefs. First, I introduce the notion of testimonial respect, understood as a relation between agent A and agent B in which A responds to B in an epistemically appropriate way, in accordance with B’s properly recognised epistemic status. Drawing on Darwall’s work on respect, I then distinguish three tiers of testimonial respect: recognition of basic rationality, recognition of individual epistemic traits, and epistemic appraisal. I show how this tripartite framework brings together a range of issues that have so far been treated separately in social epistemology, including epistemic injustice, expertise, epistemic trust, and epistemic blame. I argue that introducing epistemic respect allows for a more unified account of these phenomena and helps to reveal systematic connections between them that remain obscured on standard approaches. Finally, I outline some broader implications of this framework for our understanding of intellectual ethics and epistemic evaluation.

2.10.3. Chelsea Guo, Princeton University, Doxastic Injustice, not Wronging

A growing literature on doxastic wronging maintains that agents can wrong others merely by virtue of what they believe about them. This paper argues that while there exist beliefs that are morally troubling, they are mischaracterized as instances of moral wronging by believers.

The strongest objection to moralizing beliefs appeals to the familiar ought-implies-can principle: moral obligations apply only where compliance is within an agent’s control; because beliefs are not subject to voluntary control, there are no moral obligations owed to others to believe one way rather than another. Existing defenses of doxastic wronging either presuppose an implausibly strong form of doxastic control or blur the line between moral and epistemic normativity. Epistemic norms plausibly govern beliefs without regard to feasibility: agents can be epistemically criticizable for believing on insufficient evidence even when they could not have believed otherwise. Moral norms, by contrast, are constrained by ought-implies-can and therefore do not apply to beliefs. Agents thus cannot morally wrong others simply in virtue of what they believe.

Rejecting doxastic wronging, however, does not entail that all beliefs are morally innocent. The paper’s positive contribution is that the normativity operative in paradigmatic cases of alleged doxastic wronging is political rather than moral. Like epistemic normativity, political normativity applies to its objects without regard to feasibility.

On this view, when an agent forms a belief about another on the basis of evidence shaped by injustice, the person about whom the belief is formed is genuinely wronged. But the appropriate target of criticism is not the individual believer; it is the social structures that make such evidence available. Insofar as the state sustains such social structures, the state, not the believer, is to blame for such beliefs. And insofar as that state is democratic, responsibility for remedying doxastic injustice lies with us all.

2.10.4. Michele Bocchiola, University of Geneva, Trusting the Institutional Rule-Breaker. Office Trust Under Dirty Hands

What happens to trust among officeholders (for short ‘office trust’) when one of them deliberately violates institutional rules to secure what she regards as a higher value? This paper addresses a neglected dimension of Walzer’s dirty hands problem: its implications for intra-institutional trust relations. I argue that office trust remains warranted despite justified rule violation if and only if the officeholder acts from a 'pro officio' attitude—comprising role endorsement, norm acceptance, and office accountability. This thesis emerges from examining two competing conceptions of office trust. The reliable rule compliance view grounds trust in behavioral conformity secured by external constraints: monitoring mechanisms, incentive structures, and sanctioning practices that render deviation irrational. This view fails because it conflates trust with mere reliance and presupposes that rules always provide clear guidance—precisely what dirty hands scenarios deny. The judicious role fulfillment view offers a superior alternative by grounding trust in the officeholder’s internal stance toward her role. I establish necessity through three failure cases: removing role endorsement leaves motivational commitment detachable from institutional values; removing norm acceptance eliminates moral remainder and instrumentalizes discretion; removing office accountability renders judgment opaque and unevaluable. Sufficiency follows because these components jointly provide motivational, normative, and justificatory assurance that makes trust rational even under rule violation. An officeholder who manifests this attitude demonstrates stable commitment to institutional values, appropriate moral seriousness about violations, and publicly answerable reasoning that peers can recognize as responsive to institutional mandates. The framework handles hard counter-cases—sectarian officials, erosion by accumulation, adversarial offices—by specifying boundary conditions including minimal openness requirements and publicity tests. The analysis reveals a striking paradox: rule violation can sometimes strengthen rather than undermine trust, provided it manifests the very commitment to institutional values that trust relationships require.

2.11.2. Zhengping Zhang, University of Virginia, Toward a Relational Picture of Self-Trust

Individuals experiencing impostor syndrome raise the puzzle of simultaneously possessing and lacking sufficient self-trust in the same domain. The three plausible claims below are in tension with one another:

I. Self-trust involves domain-specific confidence in one’s competence.

II. Individuals experiencing impostor syndrome lack sufficient self-trust in domains where neutral observers would expect them to possess it.

III. Individuals experiencing impostor syndrome exhibit sufficient confidence in the same domain, manifested through their confident negative self-assessment in that domain.

This paper argues that the best way to solve the puzzle is to move from an individualistic characterization of self-trust to a relational picture. §1 and §2 explain why the three claims in the puzzle are plausible. §3 refutes the strategy of rejecting Claim III to solve the puzzle. §4 examines the second type of solution proposed by Nadja El Kassar (2019): to describe individuals with impostor syndrome as having self-trust in their self-distrust. §5 turns to Katherine Dormandy’s (2024) approach to reject Claim I and argue that her account fails to explain self-doubt. §6 argues that the better solution is to endorse a relational picture of self-trust, where self-trust consists of an individual’s perception of their relation with their salient community of audience.

2.11.4. Sandra Branzaru, University of Bucharest, To ToM or not ToM: the LLMs question

Recent debates have emerged in relation to Theory of Mind (ToM) in the context of Large Language Models (LLMs), since these have successfully completed False-Belief Tasks (FBTs). Some assume that this performance is consistent with (human) mentalistic inference (Kosinski, 2023; Strachan et. al., 2024), while others are skeptic about it (Riemer et. al.,2024).

FBTs have been originally designed within a Theory-Theory (TT) framework. Their purpose was to test if children can ascribe unobservable mental states (to other agents), that causally explain behavior. Succes in passing FBTs was interpreted as evidence that children apply a theory-like understanding of minds (Wimmer and Perner, 1983; Baron-Cohen et. al., 1985; Wellman et. al., 1990).

Unlike humans, LLMs are constrained to linguistic interactions and are usually trained on data that contains multiple examples of FBTs. Does their performance on ToM benchmarks actually suggest reliance on the same mechanism that people (allegedly) use? Or just statistical pattern recognition, mere prediction of outputs, without positing hidden causal beliefs? Behaviourally, prediction and inference can both produce the same output, even if the underlying mechanisms are fundamentally different. Assuming that LLMs pass FBTs, and therefore have ToM under TT, would be a category mistake. We would be taking their performance, which arises from statistical pattern recognition, as a mechanism which arises from inferences over hidden mental states. LLMs seem to reproduce TT mechanisms without actually implementing its postulates.

I argue that LLMs passing FBTs is not only insufficient to conclude that LLMs represent mental states, but also that this challenges the core assumptions and postulates of TT, since LLMs can pass FBTs by simply modelling the language of folk psychology. Either FBTs never measured ToM in the first place, performance on FBTs arises from multiple, different architectures, or TT is the wrong framework for ToM.

2.12.1. Alice Harberd, University College London, Closure and Mysticism

There has been widespread philosophical interest in the idea of a narrative self-conception (NSC) – a basic view of what one is like and the events of one’s life which is narrative in form. Goldie has argued that NSCs can mislead us not only about ourselves, but about the wider world. He thinks our desire for narrative closure means that NSCs can lead us to believe false, mystical explanations of why events in our lives have happened.

I argue that Goldie’s concern relies on an inadequate theory of narrative closure. This theory holds that closure is provided by the answers to narratively salient questions (Carroll, 2007). 2.12.4. Jiayue Jin, University of Leeds, Self-Trust: Saying 'I' Without Knowing

The self-reference rule (SRR) holds that, in first-person thought, ‘I’ refers to the subject who produces the thought. It is often held that a genuine user of ‘I’ must also self-consciously know that she refers to herself. However, mere awareness that one is speaking does not explain why first-person thoughts are self-conscious. Traditional accounts often end up appealing to self-consciousness itself to explain this, which leads to circularity or regress.

O’Brien’s (2007) agency account aims to avoid circularity or regress by grounding self-conscious first-person reference through agent-awareness. On this view, a subject refers to herself by knowing, as an agent, that she is forming the thought. However, the account marginalises passive, non-agential cases, in which subjects still use ‘I’ in accordance with SRR despite lacking agent-awareness.

I therefore offer a self-trust account, where the meaningful use of ‘I’ is secured through Wright’s (2004) idea of default entitlement based on self-trust. This account captures both active and passive first-personal uses in a unified way, while avoiding circularity or regress. I suggest that our capacity for first-person thought rests not merely on agency, but on the deeper constitutive principle of self-trust that warrants us to take such thoughts as our own.

2.13.2. Bennett Eckert-Kuang, Harvard University, Actions as Reasons for Actions

What are reasons for action? The dominant positions hold that ‘motivating’ reasons (i.e., reasons on the basis of which an agent acts) are facts, propositions, and/or mental states; and ‘normative’ reasons (i.e., reasons that count in favor of acting) are facts. I argue that these positions construe the ontology of reasons too narrowly. Actions themselves, I argue, can be both motivating and normative reasons. To buy groceries, for instance, can be both my motivating reason for going to the store and a normative reason for me to do so. My argument is as follows. Motivating reasons are answers to Anscombe’s ‘Why?’ question, which takes the form ‘Why are you phi-ing?’ ‘I am going to the store in order to buy groceries’ is a good answer to the question ‘Why are you going to the store?’ Further, I contend that it is not reducible to any answer that cites a proposition (e.g., ‘going to the store is a way to buy groceries’). Thus, ‘to buy groceries’ can be a motivating reason for going to the store. More generally, the end for the sake of which an agent is acting can itself be her reason for taking the means. We might say of someone: ‘her reason for going to the store is to buy groceries.’ So actions can be motivating reasons. What about normative reasons? A popular, plausible view says that normative reasons just are good motivating reasons. So if I should go to the store in order to buy groceries, then to buy groceries is a normative reason to go to the store. We might say: ‘the reason she should go to the store is to buy groceries.’ The upshot is that the dominant views about the ontology of motivating and normative reasons are false because they are too restrictive.

2.13.3. Marie Wegener, Bielefeld University, The content (in)dependence of promissory reasons

Proponents of value-based theories of practical reasons can explain different kinds of practical reasons in terms of values in various ways. Some reasons seem to be perfectly explained by appealing to the value of their favoured action. However, it seems that for other reasons, this explanation is either insufficient or plainly false, as they are not, or at least not solely, dependent on the value of the favoured action because they at least partially depend on a choice. This appears to be the case, for example, with promissory reasons, reasons to obey legitimate authority, and commitment-based reasons. One strategy to incorporate promissory reasons into the framework of a value-based theory is to provide a value-based explanation for their choice-dependence, by explaining the ability to make a promise in terms of its value. However, this explanatory strategy comes with several difficulties.

The one I focus on in this paper is the following: While it might be possible to explain the existence of the promissory reason in this way, a value-based explanation of normative powers cannot account for the different strengths of promissory reasons. One way of addressing this issue is to reject the idea that there are purely promissory-dependent reasons altogether. However, this clashes with the intuitively plausible idea that the reason to perform a promised action is simply the fact that it was promised. Another approach would be to bite the bullet and to deny that promissory reasons differ in their strength. Without a convincing explanation for why this is not the case, this seems false, as it leads to implausible implications concerning how promissory reasons behave in practical deliberation with other reasons. In this paper, I explore whether there is a third approach that preserves the idea that the reason to keep a particular promise is the fact that one promised, while also accounting for the different strengths of promissory reasons. I am working out the specific way in which promissory reasons are content-independent and thereby the sense in which they are perhaps not. Therefore, I am analyzing the validity conditions for promises to be binding. I argue that the content of the action matters in (at least) two respects: First, its promise-independent deontic status matters in a constraining sense: The action needs to be morally permissible at least. Second, it matters in the sense that the promisee's choice to make the promise and the promisor's choice to accept it must be intelligible – the promise needs to have a point. There are several ways in which a promise can have a point. One aim of my paper is to work out whether this purpose can always be spelled out in terms of the value of the action. If so, this would provide a means by which the value-based view could offer a comprehensive explanation of promissory reasons.

My work engages critically with: Heuer (2025), Raz (2019 and 2022), Riedener and Schwind (2022), and Lovett and Riedener (2024).

2.13.4. Benedict Rumbold, University of Nottingham, Comparative Unfairness as Non-Recognition

According to

The Comparative Fairness Requirement (CFR): All other things being equal, claims ought to be satisfied in proportion to their strength. (Broome, 1990, 95)

CFR looks true. But its justification is unclear. Why should we think that claims ought to be satisfied in proportion to their strength? What would be wrong about our disproportionately satisfying claims?

Somewhat surprisingly, philosophers have yet to explore these questions in depth. However, to the extent that they have reflected upon them, they have generally offered one of two views. First, we can read some writers to hold what we can call,

The Primitive View: comparatively unfair acts are wrong just because they are unfair.

Second, we can read some writers to hold what we can call,

The Concern and Respect View: Comparatively unfair acts are wrong just because they fail to treat people with equal (and sufficient) concern and respect.

2.14.1. Abida Malik, Johannes Kepler University Linz, Is Democracy Doomed? On the Importance of Epistemic Virtues

With filter bubbles and echo chambers, epistemic vices such as epistemic closed-mindedness or gullibility are thriving. 2.14.3. Moritz Schnorpfeil, LSE, Collective Traps, Fair Play, and Political Obligations

FINEGRAIN: If ⟨O,⟨p₁,…,pₘ⟩⟩=⟨O*,⟨p*₁,…,p*ₙ⟩⟩, then O=O*, m=n, and pᵢ=p*ᵢ for 1≤i≤n.

My talk examines advantages and limits of Williamson's account compared with Salmon's. Against Williamson, I first argue that propositions are not sets, since they have truth values that sets lack. Second, it is unclear which worlds are relevant for constructing his propositions: metaphysical, conceptual … or logical, and in the last case, which logic would be involved? Third, if worlds - as many theorists maintain - aggregate states of affairs, and states of affairs are entities similar to Russellian propositions, then Williamsonian propositions would ultimately presuppose Russellian ones. Fourth, although Williamson offers an appealing argument against Salmon's view that guises affect the truth conditions of belief reports, it remains unclear what those conditions are in Williamson's view. Finally, I argue that Williamson's riticism of FINEGRAIN is not compelling.

3.14. Mind and Perception [Edith Morley 126F]

3.14.1. Romanos Koutedakis, Birkbeck College, University of London, Meinongian Theories of Perception

Most assume that the bad cases of illusion and hallucination are not perceptions. What I call ‘Meinongian Theories of Perception’ disagree: in the good case, I stand in a relation to existing objects – MacBeth perceives the existing dagger – whilst in the bad case, I stand in a relation to non-existent objects – MacBeth perceives the non-existent dagger. My aim is to properly introduce Meinongian Theories of Perception, for they receive little notice in the literature. I distinguish between two versions of such theories, a psychological version, in which non-existent objects are intentional, and an apsychological version, in which non-existent objects are mind-independent. I argue that the psychological version collapses into disjunctivism, whilst the apsychological version remains a live but underexplored option. The broader lesson learned is that the ontological status of what is perceived matters for theories of perception.

3.14.2. Daniel Garcia Saavedra, University of York, Into the Heart of the Experiences of Total Darkness

Are visual experiences of total darkness illusory/hallucinatory or cases of veridical perception? I argue that they are neither. First, I argue that these experiences are not veridical because they do not fulfil the matching condition for veridically, i.e., that our experience matches what is there in the environment. Secondly, I argue that visual experiences of total darkness are neither illusory nor hallucinatory. I claim that they are not illusions because they do not misrepresent any property in the environment, and that they are not hallucinatory because they exhibit a strong dependency between the scene before our eyes and what we seem to see, something missing in all cases of hallucination. Instead, I argue that visual experiences of total darkness are indetermined visual experiences. This view, I claim, vindicates the intuitive idea that in seeing total darkness we seem to see something while failing to see anything.

3.14.3. Noddy Lam, University of Pittsburgh, On the Temporal Viewpointedness of Perceptual Experience

Hoerl (2018) recently argued that our perceptual experience lacks temporal viewpointedness. Having reconstructed his argument, I will show how his conclusion only follows if an implicit assumption is true, namely that any putative temporal perspective one occupies must be a purely perceptual perspective. I argue that we should not accept this assumption. By illustrating how our bodily awareness and psychological orientations towards different directions in space contribute to the way the perspective we occupy in visual experience is structured, I show that the latter is not a purely visual perspective, but is embedded within our overall spatial perspective. This puts pressure on Hoerl’s assumption insofar as it is an example of how features of non-visual consciousness – something more general – contribute to our visual phenomenology – something more specific. Perhaps sometimes getting right some specific phenomenon might require understanding some more general phenomenon. This opens up the possibility that, in order to get right the temporal view pointedness of perception, we need to look beyond perception and its content. I conclude by outlining an account of the overall, conscious temporal perspective that we occupy, within which perception is embedded.

3.14.4. Alice Roberts, University of Cambridge, Can Statues Make Values 'Perceptible'?

Monumental art is uncontroversially tied to a community’s values. In Art in Three Dimensions, Noël Carroll suggests a more precise connection: that monumental art makes values perceptible. In this paper, I expand on Carroll’s claim, arguing that statues can evoke a ‘complicit perception’ in a viewer. A complicit perception involves seeing the subject of the statue as having a thick ethical property. For instance, the Charlottesville statue of Robert E. Lee depicts him on horseback, looking strong and wearing a military uniform. I argue that the complicit perception evoked in this case represents Lee as-heroic. Regardless of a viewer’s beliefs, viewing the statue of Robert E Lee involves an involuntary and automatic perceptual entertainment of Lee’s heroism.

To support this argument, I draw from research on perceptual learning. I demonstrate that thick ethical properties, such as ‘heroism’, can be visually learned through frequent exposure to statues with certain visual properties (e.g., being large, grand, and elevated), combined with our cultural practices of veneration. I identify two normative consequences that stem from these perceptions. First, if perceptual experience can represent thick ethical properties, then a tension arises between what one sees and what one believes, generating dissonance that is both intrinsically and instrumentally harmful. Second, because perception is relatively insulated from belief, strategies to mitigate the impact of objectionable statues by targeting beliefs—such as adding explanatory plaques—will typically fail. Successful interventions must target perceptual experience itself.

3.15. Metaphysics and Free Will [Edith Morley 150]

3.15.1. Robyn Waller, University of Sussex, Sourcehood compatibilism requires alternatives: New perspectives on Frankfurt cases and the Garden of Forking Paths

Free will is often understood as the control an agent exercises over her actions that is required for the agent to be held morally responsible. This necessary control has been classified in the literature as of two varieties, sourcehood and leeway control. According to accounts of sourcehood, an agent must be a significant source of her actions for her to act freely. The action is ‘up to her’ or her own. She is the author of that action. According to leeway accounts, an agent must have the right kind of alternatives to her actual course of action in order for her to act freely. This is often times discussed as the ability to do otherwise. This question of kind of control relevant for moral responsibility is related to, but distinct from, whether free will is compatible with determinism, the compatibility question. Prominent contemporary sourcehood compatibilists (ala Fischer), for dialectical reasons, have often left open whether leeway control may be incompatible with determinism.

My aim in this talk is to argue for an enhanced sourcehood compatibilist view, one that takes leeway control to be required for significant sourcehood and so for significant moral responsibility, consistent with determinism. This Kane-style compatibilism view takes seriously that the exercise of free will is inextricably linked to self-expression and self-formation. To put it in terms of the garden of forking paths, our actual actions are the taken paths of self-expression, but the paths not walked signify our freedom of alternative expression, possibilities that ground our significant moral agency. To do so, I will introduce a new perspective on an old debate, the perspective of (would be) manipulators and agents themselves in Frankfurt-style cases, to establish that such cases support, not undermine, that moral responsibility requires the ability to do otherwise.

3.15.2. Jan Westerhoff, University of Oxford, Idealism and predictive processing

A plausible case can be made that the predictive processing approach to cognition incorporates central idealist positions relating to the nature of representation, the role our minds play in structuring our experience of the world, and the properties of the world behind our representations. These include the representationalist claim that we do not encounter the world in a direct manner, but only through a veil of representations, that the way the world appears to us is extensively and essentially shaped by structural features of the human mind, and the rejection of the position that there is a world of mind-independent represented objects behind our representations that corresponds, at least in broad structural outlines, to the entities featuring in contemporary physical and mathematical theories.

This paper first describes what these positions are, and how predictive processing introduces them in terms of precisely formulated scientific analogues. I will then consider the recent debate (by authors such as Schlicht and Hohwy) about whether this entails that “predictive processing harbours an inconsistent set of commitments and aspirations” and discuss how the predicitive processing approach can help us to pursue new ways of developing key idealist questions in a way that is integrated with a naturalistically supported endeavour to understand central features of reality.

3.15.3. İbrahim Hansu, LMU Munich, A Free Will Argument for DARC (Deliberation Annihilates Reflexive Credences)

Can I predict, or assign credences to, what my choice will be, while deliberating about what to choose? Some, including Alan Hájek (2016), argue yes—"deliberation welcomes prediction"—contending that the opposing thesis (DARC: Deliberation Annihilates Reflexive Credences) imposes heavy costs on Bayesian epistemology, and dismissing free will-based defenses of DARC as "wishy-washy." I offer a free will argument for DARC, formulated as a dilemma. If an agent's possible actions have determinate non-trivial probabilities, then chance-based arguments against free will (i.e. Luck objections) apply, undermining the free will that deliberation presupposes. If actions are undetermined but non-chancy—as some agent-causal theories suggest—then they have no objective probabilities (constituting objective probability gaps) to guide credences. Freedom thus introduces unpredictability, and deliberation welcomes prediction only insofar as free will is exposed to the Luck objections. I conclude by addressing purported counterexamples to DARC, including the cases of future choices, group agents, and the predictions of onlookers.

3.16. Discourse, Assertion, Memory [Edith Morley 257]

3.16.1. Johan Chung, King's College London, How to Stop Talking Past Each Other

Radical disagreements about consciousness are often dismissed as cases in which theorists are merely talking past one another. I argue that such dismissals are unjustified unless the explanandum has been fixed, and I articulate a principle governing the assessment of disagreement based on the distinction between explanandum and explanans. When this principle is applied to debates in the philosophy and science of consciousness, it shows that accounts which exclude subjective experience fail to target the same explanandum as their rivals. As a result, subjective experience plays an ineliminable role in securing genuine disagreement between theories of consciousness.

3.16.2. Ravi Thakral, University of Nevada, Reno, Socially Centered Discourse

People often preface a claim by foregrounding part of their identity: “Speaking as an American…”, “Speaking as an immigrant…”, “Speaking as an educator…”. Although these locutions are ubiquitous in everyday and public discourse, they are surprisingly under-theorized as a distinctive linguistic device. They do more than supply biographical information: they shift the conversational move the speaker is making—what authority is being claimed, which objections are apt, and what sort of uptake is invited.

Two familiar pictures suggest themselves. On a spokesperson picture, ‘speaking as an F’ is a way of speaking for Fs, or of presenting one’s claim as representative of the group. On a testimonial picture, it signals first-person evidence: the speaker is reporting something grounded in lived experience. Both capture genuine uses, but neither fits the full range. Speakers can use ‘speaking as’ while explicitly departing from what is typical of the relevant group, and challenges like ‘you do not speak for all immigrants’ can feel beside the point even when literally true. Conversely, some requests for evidence or neutrality can come off as missing what the speaker is doing.

I develop a metalinguistic account on which ‘speaking as’ negotiates two things at once. First, it positions the speaker with respect to a social category for the purposes of the conversation, sometimes tightening and sometimes loosening what counts as belonging. Second, it negotiates epistemic and normative standing: who is entitled to say what, to whom, and with what expectations of deference, contestation, or uptake. This explains why some uses are spokesperson-like while others are testimony-like, without reducing either into the other. The result is a clearer map of misalignment, rebuttal, and responsibility in socially charged discourse. I close by drawing implications for speech act theory, authority, discursive injustice, and norms of disagreement.

3.16.3. Jessie Munton, University of Cambridge, What Difference Does Remembering Make?

This paper argues for two claims. The first is that epistemic value comes in two forms: potential, and actual. The second is that attention is the gateway which mediates between them. The paper’s argument starts from the phenomenon, widely studied in psychology and neuroscience, of retrieval induced forgetting (RIF). RIF occurs when, in the course of remembering a piece of information x (a phone number for instance), the subject must suppress competing, related pieces of information, y and z, which are consequently transiently forgotten. The phenomenon of RIF reveals to us that remembering comes at a price. But we struggle to articulate what that price is with standard measures of epistemic value. I argue that we need to distinguish between potential epistemic value—the value which attaches to states we are poised to use —and actual epistemic value—the value which attaches to states we are occurrently accessing and using. In cases of RIF we trade potential epistemic value for actualised value.

This distinction has implications for another significant question at the intersection of philosophy of mind and epistemology: what is the epistemic role of attention? I argue that attention is the gateway that mediates between these forms of epistemic value. RIF is an unusually clear instance of the trade-off we face between these two sorts of epistemic value, but it is just one manifestation among many of the attentional mechanisms that govern the interface between memory and forgetting, retrieval and suppression. To understand epistemic value, the forms it takes and the cognitive achievements in which it vests, we need to pay attention to the way the mind moves through information, to the variegated nature of belief, and the transformations wrought by the mind’s engagement with information that is in some weak sense already known to it.

3.17. Moral and Normative Philosophy [Edith Morley 288]

3.17.1. Guido Melchior, University of Graz, Epistemic regresses as starting-problems and stopping-problems

In this paper, I will take a fresh look at a number of persistent epistemic regress problems, including Agrippa’s trilemma and problems of meta-justification, meta-awareness, and metaphysical explanation. Regress-problems must be divided into two groups, starting-problems that initiate a regress, and stopping-problems that attempt to end them. I will use this distinction to explore regress problems of justification, explanation, understanding, reasoning, argumentation, and belief. However, I will proceed by systematically investigating regresses of potential carriers of these epistemic phenomena – propositions, assertions, and beliefs – and the causal relations between them. I will argue that the metaphysical constraints on these regresses are substantially different. Proceeding in this way, we acquire a promising new classification of traditional epistemic regress problems and potential solutions. We can detect analogies between regresses of different epistemic phenomena with the same type of carriers but also distinguish between different regress problems for the same type of epistemic phenomena with different carriers. Moreover, asymmetries between regress problems of arguing and making someone believe and between explaining and making someone understand will be revealed.

3.17.2. Nora Grigore, Romanian Academy, Moral Normative Forces and the Problem of Supererogation

Intuitively, it seems that no justification can be required from the moral agent who does not give one's life to save others. Omissions of supererogatory actions do not seem to require justification or guilt. Then why does the problem of supererogation demand a justification for omitting morally good deeds?

I argue that thinking about the problem of supererogation (i.e. asking how can we explain that excellent moral deeds are not obligatory) may lead to distinguishing between two ways of seeing morality and its normative force. The concept of supererogation often departs from the usual way of seeing the moral domain: it makes use of obligation and duty only to state that there is a moral realm beyond duty and the usual moral obligation. It strongly suggests, therefore, a different core of morality, one that is not moral obligation.

C. Korsgaard, J. Dancy and M.O. 3.17.3. Joshua Jarvis-Campbell, University of Exeter, Animal Agriculture and the Long-Term Future

If developments in artificial intelligence continue, we may end up living in a post-scarcity society. Among the many questions that could be asked about what should be done in such a society is the following: How should we treat nonhuman animals? This paper focuses in particular on animal agriculture, asking whether we have any obligation to stop farming animals in a post-scarcity society. I argue that farming animals in a post-scarcity society could be plausibly done in a way that is morally permissible, even if the practice involves killing animals and consuming their flesh. To do this, I examine five types of objections to farming animals: welfare-based objections, wrongness-of-killing objections, respect-based objections, anthropocentric objections, and virtue ethics-based objections. 3.17.4. Charlotte Franziska Unruh, University of Southampton, Understanding the Relationship between Meaningfulness and Well-Being

Many people hold that meaningfulness and well-being are distinct dimensions of the good life. But how do these dimensions of the good life relate? In this paper, I develop a novel account, according to which the meaningfulness of our lives depends on our attitudes to things that make our lives go better. More precisely, φ is meaningful for an agent A if and only if (i) φ contributes to A’s well-being, and (ii) A has an appropriate attitude to φ, where “appropriate attitude” can be spelled out as an attitude of care (Frankfurt 1998, 2004). The novel feature of this account is that meaningfulness depends on well-being: something that does not contribute to our well-being cannot be meaningful for us. I defend this view by addressing two objections. First, I claim that cases of self-sacrifice are not counterexamples to my view, arguing that cases of self-sacrifice are most plausibly understood as cases in which agents forego an opportunity to maximize their well-being (often in favour of increased meaningfulness). Second, I defend the idea that what is meaningful for an agent depends on the agent’s subjective attitudes by showing that my account, when coupled with an objectivist account of well-being, can accommodate a range of objectivist intuitions about meaningfulness. Finally, I argue that my view is superior to alternative views on the relationship between meaningfulness and well-being that have been proposed in the literature by Metz (2007, 2012, 2013), Woodard (2017), and Hammerton (2022). I argue that my view is not vulnerable to the challenge that meaning is merely a subset of well-being, rather than a distinct category; that it maintains the powerful intuition that meaningfulness possesses final (not merely instrumental) value; and is compatible with a variety of views on the nature of well-being.
